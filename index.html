Punctuation

Many texts use only space between words and ‘᙮’ at the end of sentences as punctuation – other texts instead use a full set of Latin punctuation marks (including the Latin period). Syllabics uses a double horizontal line (᐀) as a hyphen, this is to avoid confusion with the syllable final c (◌ᐨ) used in Western Cree.

Some (Non-Pu) Words

ᑭᐱᓯ kipisi – part, portion, piece (ᑭᐱᓯ ᐁ kipisi e = cut, slice, divide)

ᒧᓐᓲᑕ monsuta – monster, beast (ᐱᓕᓐ ᒧᓐᓲᑕ pilin monsuta = fear)

ᑐᓐᓯ tonsi – non-binary gender, trans

ᓓᑯ leko – block, cube, square, stairs

ᐁᐌ ewe – gravel, soft stone, powder

ᔭᓯᒪ jasima – reflection, mirror, opposite (ᓲᐸ ᔭᓯᒪ supa jasima = mirror)

ᓕᓐᓘᐎ linluwi – web, net, network, signal, connection, to internet, connection (ᓕᓐᔭ ᓕᓐᓘᐎ linja linluwi = web, net)

ᐸᑭ taki – sticky, adhesive, clingy (ᓕᐴ ᐸᑭ lipu taki = tape)

ᐊᐯᔭ apeja – shame, guilt, embarrassment

ᒥᓯᑫᑫ misikeke – medicine, drugs

ᐗᓓᔭ waleja – context, value, topic, relating to, relevance, salience, importance

Footnotes

[1] Of course, Canadian Aboriginal syllabics is under fierce competition for the title of “coolest writing system” from other nifty scripts such as Hangul and ASLwrite.

[2] Western Cree, which were first to adopt Canadian syllabics, write w using a dot after the syllable (ᐏᐍᐘᐓ wi we wa wo), while Eastern Cree and Ojibwe, which took up syllabics later, instead put the dot in front of the syllable (ᐎᐌᐗᐒ wi we wa wo). Since Toki Pona syllabics already uses the l-series (ᓕᓓᓚᓗ li le la lo), and the final n (◌ᓐ) from Eastern Cree, I have opted to follow the Eastern Cree practice in regards to w as well, and put the w dot in front of the syllable.

[3] Since the letter j in Toki Pona is pronounced like the English y, I have chosen to use the Canadian syllabics y-series of characters (ᔨᔦᔭᔪ ji je ja jo) for j above. (Fear not, just like Toki Pona j, it is pronounced as English y.)

[4] I opted to use the l-series from Eastern Cree (also used by Inuktitut), simply because that set of glyphs respects the symmetry of the syllabics, while glyphs of the l-series in Western Cree syllabics do not follow the expected pattern of rotations. [5] The languages written in Canadian syllabics use various different glyphs for non-syllabic n. For simplicity’s sake I’m here using the character ◌ᓐ (raised ᓇ na used by Inuktitut, Eastern Cree, and the most commonly used variant in Ojibwe). Other possible choices included: ◌ᐣ (final n in Western Cree) or ◌ᣙ (raised ᓂ ni, a less commonly used variant in Ojibwe).

[6] In Canadian syllabics an overdot (ẋ) is used to mark long vowels, but Toki Pona does not make a distinction between long and short vowels. However, Canadian syllabics only have four possible rotations with which to indicate vowel, and Toki Pona has five vowels, so in this system the overdot is used to convert an o into an u, instead of its traditional vowel-lengthening role.

The o-series of glyphs – which is used here for both o (without overdot) and u (with overdot) – is used for o in most of the Canadian syllabics languages, but is used for u in Inuktitut. I’ve chosen to mark u with an overdot (rather than o) since u is the least common vowel in Toki Pona, and I want to minimize the amount of diacritics occurring in text.
