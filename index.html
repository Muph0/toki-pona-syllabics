Here is a way to write Toki Pona using Canadian syllabics! (One of the coolest writing systems!)[1] – And when you’re done reading this, you might wanna practice your Toki Pona syllabics reading skills by reading jan Pijes guide to Toki Pona (with all the Toki Pona spelled using the below system) or try out my own little Toki Pona dictionary.

  • An overdot () is used to indicate that a syllable is pronounced with u instead of o.[6]
  • Shaded syllables (ᐒ ᐔ ᑎ ᔨ) are not valid according to Toki Pona rules.

Punctuation

Instead of the usual hyphen, Canadian syllabics uses a double hyphen ‘᐀’ (this is because the final c – which is not used with Toki Pona – also looks like a hyphen).

Most texts use only spaces between words and ‘᙮’ at the end of sentences. Other texts instead use the punctuation from the Latin script (including period).

Some (Non-Pu) Words

ᑭᐱᓯ – part, portion, piece (ᑭᐱᓯ ᐁ = cut, slice, divide)

ᒧᓐᓲᑕ monster, beast (ᐱᓕᓐ ᒧᓐᓲᑕ = fear)

ᑐᓐᓯ non-binary gender, trans

ᓓᑯ block, cube, square, stairs

ᐁᐌ gravel, soft stone, powder

ᔭᓯᒪ reflection, mirror, opposite (ᓲᐸ ᔭᓯᒪ = mirror)

ᓕᓐᓘᐎ web, net, network, signal, connection, to internet, connection (ᓕᓐᔭ ᓕᓐᓘᐎ = web, net)

ᐸᑭ sticky, adhesive, clingy (ᓕᐴ ᐸᑭ = tape)

ᐊᐯᔭ shame, guilt, embarrassment

ᒥᓯᑫᑫ medicine, drugs

ᐗᓓᔭ context, value, topic, relating to, relevance, salience, importance

Footnotes

[1] Of course, Canadian Aboriginal syllabics is under fierce competition for the title of “coolest writing system” from other nifty scripts such as Hangul and ASLwrite.

[2] Western Cree, which were first to adopt Canadian syllabics, write w using a dot after the syllable (ᐏ ᐍ ᐘ ᐓ), while Eastern Cree and Ojibwe, which took up syllabics later, instead put the dot in front of the syllable (ᐎ ᐌ ᐗ ᐒ). Since Toki Pona syllabics already uses the l-series (ᓕ ᓓ ᓚ ᓗ), and the final n (◌ᓐ) from Eastern Cree, I have opted to follow the Eastern Cree practice in regards to w as well, and put the w dot in front of the syllable.

[3] In accordance with the Toki Pona way of spelling, I have labeled the y-series (ᔨ ᔦ ᔭ ᔪ) as j- in the table above. (Fear not, just like Toki Pona j, it is pronounced y.)

[4] I opted to use the l-series from Eastern Cree (also used by Inuktitut), simply because that set of glyphs respects the symmetry of the syllabics, while glyphs of the l-series in Western Cree syllabics do not follow the expected pattern of rotations. The table below show a comparison between Eastern and Western l-series (Yes! The ‘Western’ column looks all wonky, but I assure you: That is not a mistake on my part!):

[5] The languages written in Canadian syllabics use various different glyphs for non-syllabic n. For simplicity’s sake I’m here using the character ◌ᓐ (used by Inuktitut, Eastern Cree, and the most common variant in Ojibwe). Other possible choices would’ve included ◌ᐣ (used in Western Cree) or ◌ᣙ (a variant from Ojibwe which is more rarely seen).

[6] In Canadian syllabics the overdot () is used to mark long vowels, but Toki Pona does not make a distinction between long and short vowels. However, Canadian syllabics only have four possible rotations with which to indicate vowel, and Toki Pona has five vowels, so in this system the overdot is instead used to convert an o into an u, instead of its traditional vowel-lengthening role.

The o-series of glyphs – which is used here for both o (without overdot) and u (with overdot) – is used for o in most of the Canadian syllabics languages, but is used for u in Inuktitut. I’ve chosen to mark u with an overdot (rather than o) since u is the least common vowel in Toki Pona, and I want to minimize the amount of diacritics occurring in text.
